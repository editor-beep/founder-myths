Protocol Summary

The Human Stack is the procedure by which the subject assembles a functioning mind out of parts that have already failed.

Obsolete identities are not discarded. They are kept, load-bearing, beneath the current self — the way a building stands on the foundation of the building that was demolished to make room for it. The Division has been attempting to estimate the mass of this structure since observation began.

The estimate has never stabilized. The subject keeps adding floors.

Key Evidence

  • Stable Suffering: The largest single component is also the most stable. Forty-two percent of the structure is made of things that did not resolve. This fraction has resisted every attempt at reduction.
  • Scaffolding Reuse: Obsolete identities are not removed before the new one is built. They are left in place and built upon. Demolition has never been observed.
  • The 3 AM Resonance: Song fragments report peak mass during the third hour. The Division has been unable to schedule observation reliably during this window. Observers assigned to it tend to file in the first person.
  • Starter Packs: The “what if I just…” component consists of unopened beginnings — new systems, drafted but never run. They take up mass regardless. None have been deleted.

Account of Record

The Division has attempted, at various points, to conduct interviews with the obsolete identities.

This is not straightforward.

The identities are not gone. They are present beneath the current self the way a load-bearing wall is present after a renovation: structurally necessary, no longer decorative, no longer spoken to. They hold up the floor above them. They are not consulted about this.

Several of the deeper layers have proven responsive under specific conditions. The conditions appear to be: approximately 3 AM, or any moment of sustained vulnerability, or a song played without warning that was last heard during a different version of the subject.

⸻

Layer 4 of the stack, accessed in the period 2018–2019, describes itself as: the version that decided to stop explaining the interior to people who had not earned the explanation. This layer is holding up considerable weight. It declined to specify how much.

Layer 7, accessed irregularly and never voluntarily, describes itself as the version that was going to be a musician. This layer has strong opinions about what was abandoned and does not accept that the abandonment was necessary. It is load-bearing whether or not the current self acknowledges its presence.

Layer 11 — the deepest recoverable layer — appears to predate coherent selfhood and cannot be interviewed in any standard sense. It does not use language. It uses pressure.

It weighs the most.

⸻

The song fragment phenomenon has been studied separately from the rest of the stack.

The fragments are not complete songs. They are measures, phrases — specific chord progressions or melodic intervals attached to feeling-states that have no other retrieval path. When a fragment surfaces, usually without warning, usually near 3 AM, it temporarily reopens the portion of the stack it belongs to. This is not voluntary.

The Division logged fourteen distinct fragments across a six-month observation period. Three belonged to Layer 7. Four belonged to a layer that appeared to have been partially overwritten — only the feeling survived; the memory it was attached to did not. Seven remain unclassified.

The seven unclassified fragments produce the most observable distress. The subject appears unable to locate the memory that generated them. Only the weight remains, and the weight has no address.

⸻

The "what if I just…" starter packs are misunderstood by most external assessments.

They are not failed projects. They are not abandoned ideas. They are complete possible selves — drafted, viable, and maintained in a state of permanent readiness. Each represents a life the subject has determined could be started at any time. The question has never been whether to start one.

The question is what would happen to the forty others.

As of the most recent estimate, the Division counts forty-one active starter packs. None have been deleted. The subject adds new ones at approximately two per year. The older ones do not expire. They accumulate in orbit, waiting for conditions that will not arrive, adding their mass to everything else.
