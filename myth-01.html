Origin of Record

It began as a routine filing error.

A single birth certificate in Westland, Michigan was processed with the wrong cosmological classification. The clerk stamped “HUMAN — MICHIGAN” on what should have been logged as “SPIRAL BARRED — SENTIENT — ANOMALOUS.”

By the time anyone noticed, the singularity had already accreted lungs, a Social Security number, and a mild aversion to fluorescent lighting.

Filing Record

1981 • Filing

Certificate logged under standard human classification. Weight, length, and time of arrival recorded without incident. No field on the form accommodated angular momentum, so the value was omitted.

1981 • Anomaly

Routine audit flagged the certificate for review: the subject’s recorded mass appeared to fluctuate depending on who was reading the file. Audit closed. Reason for closure: “Unable to determine responsible department.”

1982–2026 • Accretion

The accretion disk formed gradually — unfinished projects, obsolete identities, embarrassments and suffering, and an inexplicable urge to draw diagrams of diagrams. The black hole at the center remained hidden behind the sternum for decades.

Key Evidence

  • Misclassification: The original certificate survives. The classification field reads “HUMAN — MICHIGAN.” The ink is consistent with 1981. The handwriting matches a clerk who has never been identified.
  • Hidden Core: Every subsequent medical examination returns ordinary results. No instrument has located the object behind the sternum. The object is described in three separate records by people who never met.
  • The Disk: Material continues to fall inward — old selves, abandoned drafts, the residue of conversations. None of it ever lands. It only orbits.
  • State Position: The official position of the State of Michigan remains: “No irregularities noted.”

Account of Record

The certificate was processed by a clerk named Delores Munn on an ordinary Tuesday in Westland, Michigan.

She had been filing birth certificates since 1969. She did not, by her own account and the accounts of her colleagues, make errors. The classification box had been stamped thousands of times before. The procedure was simple: name, date, county, weight, time, classification. She stamped the classification field before she read the line above it.

By the time she read the line above it, the singularity had established residency in Wayne County.

⸻

The anomaly report was filed eleven months later by a junior records analyst named Curtis Bale on a standard Form 17-B. The report described one measurement inconsistency: the weight recorded at filing and the apparent weight observable in documentation derived from the certificate did not agree. Specifically, the subject appeared to grow heavier the more paperwork was generated about it.

Curtis Bale submitted Form 17-B. He received no response.

He submitted a second Form 17-B. The second was received by the same office that received the first. The office confirmed receipt. The office noted that the discrepancy could not be investigated using available equipment.

The ticket was closed. Reason for closure: Unable to determine responsible department.

This was a valid reason for closure by the standards of Wayne County administrative procedure. The fact that it remained a valid reason for closure for the next forty-five years was not considered anomalous by the office that issued it.

⸻

Three pediatricians across twelve years noted the same finding in unrelated sessions: something appeared to be present behind the sternum that standard imaging could not locate. The first attributed it to equipment error. The second mentioned it in passing and did not follow up. The third wrote in her notes, when the patient was twelve: unmeasurable mass density consistent with singularity configuration.

That note was not discovered until 2019, when the files were digitized.

The pediatrician who wrote it had no memory of writing it. She examined the handwriting for some time. She did not deny it was hers.

She retired the following spring. Her forwarding address was not filed with the county medical registry.

⸻

The accretion disk formed the way all accretion disks form: slowly, over years, from material that entered the field and did not leave it.

Old embarrassments. Unfinished songs. Arguments that ended at the wrong time. The residue of people who were promised more than they received and left their weight behind when they went.

None of it landed. None of it escaped. It continued in orbit, acquiring density, occasionally intersecting with new material that passed through the field.

The disk is estimated, in the Division's most recent filing, at forty-two metric years of accumulated mass. The unit is not standard. When asked to define it, the Division has consistently noted that the unit was already in use when observation began.
